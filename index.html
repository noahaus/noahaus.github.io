About

My name is Noah Legall, PhD. Professionally, I would describe myself as a Bioinformatic Scientist with an extensive background in bioinformatics, comparative bacterial genomics, and data analysis + visualization. I discovered the discipline in 2014, my first year at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill, where I was introduced to bioinformatic research and the common tools utilized to analyze sequence data. I followed my love of bioinformatics and pursued my Ph. D. at the University of Georgia, where I received a formal education in theory and practice of bioinformatic analysis. During my internship, I interfaced with building bioinformatic software during my experiences, and was aware at the level of dedication it would take to make a truly cross-platform and extensible solution to analyze raw data inputs. This lead me to discovering the Nextflow platform, which alleviated every concern I had developed since my training began. Because of that, I was able to lead the development of my own pipelines to analyze genomic variability within wildlife bacterial diseases.

My Bioinformatics Journey

I've been a practitioner of bioinformatics since 2014. My earliest projects were based on the analysis of non-coding RNA that fused their introns to create circular structures (circRNA). From my first year to last year of undergrad, I was able to utilize Python and R to establish that genome wide, these sequences had a high amount of ALU repeat elements within the intronic regions, signifying a potential signature of these non-coding RNA's formation.

I've had a blessed career, giving me opportunities to perfect my craft as a research intern at various research institutions. At Boyce Thompson Institute located in Cornell I investigated nucleotide variants that impact the evolution of apple taste through malic acid accumulation. These efforts went into a larger effort describig the evolution of domesticated apples, which can be read here. I also was able to contribute to a large multi-omic effort at the University of Georgia to understand the biosignatures of malaria infection using rheus macaques as the model organism. My efforts were to help the team develop SQL queries to populate XML template files that would ultimately be used to deposit large amounts of metadata to sequence repositories.

After my undergraduate degree, I spent the next 4.5 years purusing my Ph. D. in Bioinformatics at the University of Georgia. It was here that I developed a more rigourous approach to investigating biological phenomena through the use of Next Generation Sequencing (NGS). I focused my expertise on analyzing bacterial pathogens through a comparative genomics lens. My projects, to name a few, had me build and maintain software to analyze the entire gene repertoirs of the bacterial pathogen Mycobacterium bovis usig Nextflow, utilizing explainable machine learning algorithms to investigate genes that are crucial for local and global adaptation, and global investigations of genetic variability of M. bovis. I was able to successfully defend my Ph. D. on March 14th 2023, cementing me as Dr. Noah Legall at family gatherings henceforth.

As for my current activities, I am exploring wet lab science as a Microbial Ecologist, studying the interactions of antagonistic bacteria against the model algae Chlamydomonas reinhardtii. While this is a departure from my usual skillset, I took the role because I want to be a master of biology - not just bioinformatics. Still in this Postdoctoral Researcher role, I am able to buld skills in analyzing metabolomic data generated through my experiments, and I am additionally leading analysis of Pacbio long read sequence data generated from various algal species, to detect patterns of abiotic stress at the molecular scale. Whatever happens after, I am excited for the challenge and opportunity!
